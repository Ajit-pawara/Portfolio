Incident Response Lifecycle

When a breach happens, there's no time to learn. Today you drill the NIST IR lifecycle — Preparation, Detection, Containment, Eradication, Recovery, Lessons Learned.

THEORY

The NIST SP 800-61 IR Lifecycle: Incident response is not a single action — it's a structured process with six phases. NIST SP 800-61 (Computer Security Incident Handling Guide) defines the gold standard. The lifecycle approach ensures that responders don't skip critical steps under pressure. Every phase has specific goals, actions, and success criteria.

Preparation (the most neglected phase): Most organizations fail at IR before an incident even occurs because they skip preparation. Preparation includes: documented IR plan with defined roles and communication channels, pre-deployed forensic tools (imaging stations, write-blockers, analysis VMs), established chain-of-custody procedures, and regular tabletop exercises. The time to configure logging is not after you detect a breach — it's during preparation.

Detection and Analysis: This is where the SOC operates. Alerts from EDR, SIEM, NIDS, and user reports are triaged. The key question is not "is this malicious?" but "what is the scope?" — which systems are affected, which accounts, what data, and how did the attacker get in. The analysis phase often reveals a larger compromise than initially suspected.

Containment, Eradication, and Recovery: Containment is divided into short-term (e.g., isolate the host from the network) and long-term (e.g., rebuild from clean media). Eradication removes all traces of the attacker — deleted malware, revoked certificates, rotated credentials. Recovery restores business operations, often from verified clean backups. The sequence matters: containment before eradication prevents the attacker from re-establishing access faster than you can remove it.

# Example: IR Phase Decision Tree for Ransomware 1. DETECTION: Alert: "File encryption detected on host 10.0.1.45" 2. TRIAGE: Is this a real ransomware event? Check file extensions (.locked), ransom note presence (README.txt), process list (encrypt.exe). 3. CONTAIN: Isolate host: block at switch port, disable network interface. Preserve evidence: capture memory (LiME), create disk image (dd). 4. ERADICATE: Identify initial vector (phishing? RDP brute-force?). Remove malware, patch exploited service, rotate all credentials. 5. RECOVER: Restore from verified clean backup. Validate data integrity. 6. LEARN: What failed? Write post-mortem. Implement missing control.
Why the lifecycle approach matters: Without a structured process, responders skip containment and go straight to eradication — then realize the attacker already re-infected through a backdoor that was never found. The NIST lifecycle forces methodical response, which is why every SOC job interview includes questions about it.
// attacker vs defender mindset
ATTACKER
  • Time the attack for weekends or holidays when IR teams are at minimum staffing — slower containment means more time to encrypt and exfiltrate data.
  • Deploy multiple persistence mechanisms — scheduled tasks, WMI subscriptions, services, startup folders — so that even if one is found during eradication, another remains.
  • Destroy forensic evidence before defenders can collect it — use event log clearing (wevtutil), timestomping, and secure deletion to complicate the lessons-learned phase.
  • Move fast during containment — if you detect network isolation, trigger your backup C2 channel immediately before the first connection is cut.
DEFENDER
  • Run tabletop exercises quarterly — simulate a ransomware scenario with all stakeholders (IT, legal, PR, executives). The discovery of gaps during a drill is infinitely cheaper than during a real incident.
  • Pre-deploy forensic collection tools: write-blockers, imaging workstations, and incident response jump bags with pre-configured laptops containing Volatility, FTK Imager, and chain-of-custody forms.
  • Document every action during containment with timestamps — who did what, when, and what was the result. This documentation is essential for post-incident analysis and potential legal proceedings.
  • Establish a warm site for the IR team — a separate management plane that isn't affected by the incident, with out-of-band communication (Signal, Slack, dedicated IR channel).
// real named incident
2021 — COLONIAL PIPELINE — $4.4M RANSOM PAID
Colonial Pipeline's IR team followed the NIST lifecycle to respond to the Darkside ransomware attack
On May 6, 2021, Darkside ransomware encrypted Colonial Pipeline's billing systems. The IR team executed the NIST lifecycle: Containment — immediately isolated the OT pipeline control network from the compromised IT billing network, preventing the spread to operational technology. Eradication — identified the initial vector (a single compromised VPN password without MFA) and rebuilt systems from clean backups. Recovery — resumed pipeline operations after 6 days, restoring 5,500 miles of pipeline delivering 100 million gallons/day. The lessons learned led to mandatory MFA for all remote access, network segmentation between IT and OT, and dedicated ICS incident response procedures. Colonial paid $4.4M in ransom but recovered $2.3M via DOJ seizure.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: apply the NIST IR lifecycle to a simulated ransomware scenario — you are the incident responder.

  1. Set up a scenario: create a simple IR playbook document for ransomware. Use a template from github.com/OWASP/IR-Playbooks or write your own with sections for each phase.
  2. Preparation phase: list 5 tools you would have ready before an incident (e.g., FTK Imager, KAPE, Velociraptor, a pre-configured analysis VM, and an out-of-band communication channel).
  3. Detection phase: download a free ransomware simulation tool (e.g., RanSim from KnowBe4) or use a YARA rule to search for known ransomware indicators in the /tmp directory: yara -s /path/to/rule.yar /tmp.
  4. Containment phase: write a script that, when executed on a Linux host, isolates it from the network by disabling the network interface and adding iptables rules to block all traffic except to the SIEM: iptables -A INPUT -s SIEM_IP -j ACCEPT; iptables -A INPUT -j DROP.
  5. Eradication phase: document the steps to identify and remove persistence mechanisms — check crontab (crontab -l), systemd services (systemctl list-units --type=service), and SSH authorized_keys.
  6. Lessons learned: write a 5-point post-mortem document identifying what went wrong, what went well, and 3 action items to improve. This is the deliverable.
Deliverable: a complete IR post-mortem document (1 page) covering containment actions, eradication steps, and lessons learned. This is exactly what a SOC analyst would produce after a real incident.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "Walk me through the NIST incident response lifecycle."
A: "The NIST SP 800-61 lifecycle has six phases. Preparation: having the plan, tools, and training ready before an incident. Detection and Analysis: identifying the incident through alerts, logs, and user reports, then determining the scope. Containment: stopping the incident from spreading — short-term containment like host isolation, then long-term containment like network segmentation. Eradication: removing the threat — deleting malware, closing backdoors, rotating credentials. Recovery: restoring operations from clean backups and validating functionality. Lessons Learned: conducting a post-mortem to identify what went wrong and what to improve."
Q: "How do you prioritize containment during a ransomware incident?"
A: "First, I'd isolate the affected host from the network immediately — disconnect the network cable or disable the switch port. Second, I'd preserve evidence by taking a memory dump and disk image before any cleanup. Third, I'd identify the scope — does this affect other hosts, file shares, or domain controllers? If encryption is detected on network shares, I'd block SMB traffic at the firewall. Fourth, I'd communicate to stakeholders — what systems are down, what's the estimated impact, and is law enforcement notification required. Containment is about stopping the bleed while preserving forensic integrity."
Q: "What's the most common mistake organizations make in incident response?"
A: "Skipping the Preparation phase. Most organizations try to build their IR program during the incident. They don't have pre-deployed tools, documented procedures, or defined roles. This leads to chaotic response, lost evidence, and missed containment windows. The second most common mistake is moving straight from detection to eradication without proper containment — attackers often have multiple backdoors, and if you clean one without containing the environment, they'll still be present through another."
