Security+ Domain 5 + Practice Test

Domain 5 covers governance, risk, and compliance. Today you master policies, risk management, and privacy concepts, then take a full-length practice test.

THEORY

Domain 5 — Governance, Risk & Compliance (14%): The smallest domain on the exam but critically important for real-world security roles. Topics include: security policies (acceptable use, data handling, password, change management), risk management types (qualitative vs quantitative, risk register, risk matrix), risk responses (accept, transfer, mitigate, avoid), business continuity planning (BCP, RTO, RPO, MTBF, MTTR), compliance frameworks (GDPR, HIPAA, PCI DSS, FISMA, SOX, GLBA), data privacy (PII, PHI, data sovereignty, data retention/destruction), vendor due diligence (third-party risk, SLA review), and security awareness training (phishing simulations, annual training, role-based training).

GDPR (EU)
General Data Protection Regulation — applies to any organization handling EU citizen data. Fines up to 4% of global annual revenue or €20M. Requires breach notification within 72 hours, DPO appointment, data subject access rights.
HIPAA (US Healthcare)
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — protects PHI (Protected Health Information). Requires BAA agreements, access controls, audit logs, encryption, and breach notification. Fines up to $1.5M per violation category.
PCI DSS (Payment Cards)
Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard — 12 requirements including firewall config, encryption of cardholder data, access control, monitoring, and regular testing. Required for any organization processing credit cards.
FISMA (US Government)
Federal Information Security Modernization Act — requires federal agencies to implement security programs, categorize data by FIPS 199, and undergo annual NIST RMF assessments.
SOX (Public Companies)
Sarbanes-Oxley Act — requires public companies to maintain internal controls over financial reporting. IT controls (access, change management, backup) must be documented and audited.
Risk Management
ALE = SLE × ARO (Annualized Loss Expectancy). Qualitative uses High/Medium/Low. Quantitative uses monetary values. Risk = Likelihood × Impact. Risk register tracks all identified risks.

Risk calculation example:

A server has an Asset Value (AV) of $50,000 Exposure Factor (EF) = 40% (estimated damage if exploited) Single Loss Expectancy (SLE) = AV × EF = $50,000 × 0.4 = $20,000 Annual Rate of Occurrence (ARO) = 0.2 (once every 5 years) Annualized Loss Expectancy (ALE) = SLE × ARO = $20,000 × 0.2 = $4,000 If the cost of the security control is $3,000/year and reduces ARO to 0.05: New ALE = $20,000 × 0.05 = $1,000 Saving = $4,000 - $1,000 - $3,000 = $0/year — still worth it for non-monetary factors
Key exam tip: Domain 5 questions often ask you to identify the correct risk response strategy. If a risk is low-likelihood and low-impact, you might accept it. If it's high-impact, you mitigate (reduce likelihood/impact). If a third party has better expertise, you transfer (insurance). If it can't be managed, you avoid (shut down the activity).
// attacker vs defender mindset
⚔ Attacker
  • Attackers target the gap between policy and practice — an organization may have a data retention policy, but if they don't enforce it, years of sensitive data accumulate on unmonitored servers.
  • Third-party vendors are a favorite attack vector (Target 2013, SolarWinds 2020). Attackers exploit vendor due diligence gaps — if the vendor is secure but the client doesn't audit them, the attacker uses the vendor as an entry point.
  • Compliance-driven security often creates check-the-box behavior — meeting minimum requirements without actually being secure. Attackers exploit checkbox security.
🛡 Defender
  • Document everything — policies, procedures, risk assessments, incident reports. In a breach, your documentation (or lack of it) determines regulatory penalties.
  • Perform business impact analysis (BIA) to identify critical systems and their maximum tolerable downtime (MTD). This drives RTO and RPO requirements.
  • Don't just meet compliance minimums — use frameworks as a baseline, not a target. PCI DSS compliance doesn't mean you're secure; it means you've met 12 minimum requirements.

2024 — SECURITY+ EXAM PASSING SCORE — DOMAIN WEIGHTING ANALYSIS
The Security+ exam requires 750/900. Domain weighting: 24% attacks, 21% architecture, 25% implementation, 16% operations, 14% governance
Understanding passing score mechanics is crucial for exam strategy. The Security+ SY0-701 exam has a scaled scoring range of 100-900 with a passing score of 750. This isn't a percentage — it's a scaled score adjusted for exam difficulty. You can miss approximately 15-18 questions and still pass, assuming you answer PBQs well (they're weighted highest). Domain 5 at 14% means you can afford to be weakest on governance content if you're strong on Domains 1-4, but don't ignore it entirely — 14% is still ~13 questions. Study tip: Domain 5 concepts (risk assessment types, compliance frameworks, data privacy) are memorization-heavy and low-complexity. They're the easiest points on the exam if you study them. Spend 1-2 hours on Domain 5 for high ROI on exam score.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "What's the difference between a policy, a standard, a procedure, and a guideline?"
A: "A policy is a high-level statement of management intent — 'All data must be encrypted at rest.' A standard defines specific technologies or configurations — 'Use AES-256 encryption.' A procedure is the step-by-step how-to — 'Navigate to Settings → Encryption → Enable BitLocker.' A guideline is a recommendation, not mandatory — 'Consider using hardware-backed encryption for improved performance.' Policies and standards are mandatory. Guidelines are optional best practices."
Q: "Your company experiences a data breach involving EU customer data. What regulations apply and what must you do?"
A: "If EU customer PII is involved, GDPR applies. Under GDPR, we must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach. If the breach poses a high risk to data subjects' rights and freedoms, we must also notify affected individuals without undue delay. We'd need to document the breach, its effects, and remediation steps. We might also have PCI DSS obligations if payment data was involved. Failure to notify within 72 hours risks fines up to €20M or 4% of global revenue."
Q: "How do you decide whether to accept, transfer, mitigate, or avoid a risk?"
A: "I evaluate the risk level (likelihood × impact) against the organization's risk appetite. If the cost of mitigation exceeds the potential loss and the risk is within appetite, I recommend acceptance (documented approval required). If a third party can manage the risk better, I recommend transfer — typically through cyber insurance or vendor management. If the risk exceeds appetite, I recommend mitigation — reducing likelihood or impact through controls. Avoidance is for risks that can't be managed — we shut down the activity entirely."
