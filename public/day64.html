Threat Intelligence Basics

You can't defend against what you don't know is coming. Today you learn how threat intelligence feeds work, how TTPs are categorized, and how to operationalize IoCs.

THEORY

What is Threat Intelligence? Threat intelligence is evidence-based knowledge about existing or emerging threats to your organization. It transforms raw data — IP addresses, domains, file hashes — into actionable context: who is targeting you, what tools they use, which vulnerabilities they exploit, and how to detect them. Intelligence operates on a hierarchy: Data → Information → Intelligence, where context and analysis are what turn a list of IPs into a defensive plan.

The Pyramid of Pain: David Bianco's model shows how difficult each indicator type is for an attacker to change. Hash values (easy to change) sit at the bottom — they're the least painful for attackers. TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, Procedures) sit at the top — changing TTPs is costly for attackers and most painful.

IoCs (Indicators of Compromise)
Artifacts observed on a network or system that indicate a breach — IP addresses, domains, file hashes, registry keys, mutex names, email subject lines. Machine-readable, immediately actionable.
TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, Procedures)
The behavior patterns of adversaries — how they gain initial access, move laterally, escalate privileges, exfiltrate data. More stable than IoCs but harder to detect automatically.
Threat Feeds
Continuous streams of threat data. Open feeds (AlienVault OTX, AbuseIPDB, VirusTotal, Spamhaus) provide broad coverage. Commercial feeds (Recorded Future, Anomali, CrowdStrike) offer higher fidelity and context.

STIX and TAXII: STIX (Structured Threat Information Expression) is the JSON-based language for describing threat intelligence — it standardizes how IoCs, attack patterns, campaigns, and threat actors are represented. TAXII (Trusted Automated Exchange of Intelligence Resources) is the protocol for sharing STIX data between organizations. Together, they enable automated threat sharing across sectors — the same mechanism that allowed 1,000+ organizations to share ransomware IoCs during the 2021 Kaseya supply-chain attack.

# Example: STIX 2.1 indicator (simplified) { "type": "indicator", "name": "Malicious IP - C2 Server", "indicator_types": ["malicious-activity"], "pattern": "[ipv4-addr:value = '185.130.5.193']", "pattern_type": "stix", "valid_from": "2024-01-15T00:00:00Z", "kill_chain_phases": [{ "kill_chain_name": "mitre-attack", "phase_name": "command-and-control" }] }

Operationalizing Intelligence: The intelligence lifecycle has six phases: Requirements → Collection → Processing → Analysis → Dissemination → Feedback. Raw feeds are useless without analysis — a SOC drowning in alerts needs context (priority, relevance, confidence) before an IP block becomes actionable. The best threat intelligence programs focus on questions like "What TTPs would our attackers use against our specific industry?" rather than consuming every feed available.

Why this matters: Threat intelligence turns reactive security into proactive defense. A SOC with no threat intel waits for alerts to fire. A SOC with threat intel anticipates adversary behavior, configures detection rules before the first breach, and prioritizes patching for CVEs actively being exploited in their sector.
// attacker vs defender mindset
ATTACKER
  • Monitor public threat intelligence feeds to see if your own C2 infrastructure has been listed — if it has, rotate IPs and domains before defenders can operationalize the block.
  • Use living-off-the-land binaries (LOLBins) that don't match typical malware IoCs — powershell.exe, certutil.exe, wmic.exe are trusted processes and rarely trigger IoC-based detection.
  • Deliberately vary TTPs between campaigns — change encryption methods, C2 protocols, and lateral movement techniques to avoid pattern matching against known TTP databases.
  • Feed false intelligence into open-source platforms (VirusTotal, AlienVault) by uploading benign files with fake malicious labels, polluting defender IoC databases.
DEFENDER
  • Subscribe to sector-specific ISACs (Information Sharing and Analysis Centers) — FS-ISAC for finance, Health-ISAC for healthcare — where members share timely, high-fidelity IoCs.
  • Deploy MISP (Malware Information Sharing Platform) as your local threat intel hub — aggregate multiple feeds, correlate IoCs, automate blocklist pushes to firewalls and EDR.
  • Prioritize TTP-based detection over IoC-based — TTPs are harder for attackers to change. Detect certutil.exe downloading from an external URL (TTP) rather than blocking individual domains.
  • Calculate confidence scores for each IoC source — a known APC from a sector ISAC has higher confidence than a VirusTotal submission from an anonymous user.

2021 — MISP IN KASEYA SUPPLY-CHAIN ATTACK — 1,500+ ORGANIZATIONS
MISP threat sharing platform enabled real-time ransomware IoC exchange during the Kaseya VSA supply-chain breach
When REvil exploited a Kaseya VSA zero-day (CVE-2021-30116) to deploy ransomware to MSP customers, the MISP threat sharing platform became the central clearinghouse for IoCs. Organizations using MISP received STIX-format indicators within hours — C2 IPs, registry keys, file paths, YARA rules — allowing them to block the attack before their own Kaseya agent was compromised. Over 1,500 organizations shared and consumed threat data through MISP during the incident. The rapid intelligence sharing reduced the average detection time from days to hours, proving that a connected threat intelligence community can materially reduce breach impact.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: set up MISP threat intelligence platform, consume a live STIX feed, and operationalize IoCs into firewall block rules.

  1. Deploy MISP using Docker: docker run -d -p 443:443 -p 80:80 --name misp coolacid/misp-docker. Wait 2-3 minutes for initialization.
  2. Access MISP at https://localhost (default creds: admin@admin.test / admin). Navigate to Sync Actions → List Feeds.
  3. Enable the CIRCL OSINT feed — this provides open-source threat intelligence on known malicious IPs, domains, and hashes. Click "Fetch and store all feed events."
  4. Browse the fetched events — examine one malicious IP event: note the STIX pattern, the kill chain phase (MITRE ATT&CK mapping), and the associated tags (TLP, confidence, target sector).
  5. Create a new event by manually adding an IoC: Navigate to Event Actions → Add Event. Add an indicator for a known test IP (e.g., 203.0.113.5) with the ATT&CK technique T1071 (Application Layer Protocol).
  6. Export the events in STIX 2.1 JSON format — navigate to Event Actions → Export → Download as STIX 2.1. Examine the structure.
Deliverable: A screenshot of MISP dashboard showing at least one fetched feed event and one manually created event with MITRE ATT&CK mapping. This proves you can ingest, create, and export structured threat intelligence.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "What is the difference between IoCs and TTPs, and why does it matter?"
A: "IoCs are specific artifacts like IP addresses, domains, and file hashes that indicate a compromise has occurred. TTPs describe the attacker's behavior — how they gain access, move laterally, and exfiltrate data. TTPs are more valuable for defense because they're harder for attackers to change. An attacker can rotate IPs in minutes but changing their core tradecraft takes significant effort. The Pyramid of Pain illustrates this: hash values at the bottom (easy to change), TTPs at the top (most painful to change)."
Q: "How would you operationalize a threat intelligence feed in a SOC?"
A: "First, ingest the feed into a platform like MISP or Anomali that normalizes the data. Second, correlate incoming IoCs against our environment — do any of these malicious IPs appear in our firewall logs? Have any of these hashes been seen on our endpoints? Third, prioritize based on confidence scoring and relevance to our industry. Fourth, automate response: push confirmed IoCs to firewall blocklists, SIEM correlation rules, and endpoint detection policies. Finally, close the loop by providing feedback on which IoCs were actually useful, which improves the feed's quality over time."
Q: "What are STIX and TAXII, and why were they created?"
A: "STIX is a standardized JSON language for describing threat intelligence — it defines how to represent IoCs, attack patterns, threat actors, campaigns, and course of action in a machine-readable format. TAXII is the protocol for sharing STIX data over HTTPS. They were created because organizations couldn't share threat intelligence efficiently — everyone used different formats. STIX/TAXII enables automated, real-time threat sharing across sectors, which was critical during incidents like the Kaseya attack where 1,500+ organizations shared IoCs within hours."
AI TOOLS
Threat intel report summarizer
Paste a lengthy threat report (Mandiant, CrowdStrike, Dragos) into an LLM and ask for: key IoCs, TTPs mapped to MITRE ATT&CK, and recommended detection rules.
STIX pattern generator
Describe an indicator in plain language and have an LLM output the equivalent STIX 2.1 pattern. Saves time when manually creating MISP events.
YARA rule generator
Describe malware behavior (e.g., "a file that decrypts strings at runtime and connects to a Telegram bot API") and get a candidate YARA rule to test against samples.

ENTREPRENEUR ANGLE

Threat intelligence consulting is a lucrative niche because most mid-sized organizations know they need threat intel but can't afford full-time analysts. A consulting practice offering "Threat Intel Program Setup" — deploying MISP, configuring feeds, writing standard operating procedures for IoC handling — can command $10,000-25,000 per engagement. Many MSSPs (Managed Security Service Providers) subcontract threat intelligence work because building in-house expertise is expensive. A productized opportunity: "Weekly Threat Brief" subscription — $500/month for a curated intelligence summary relevant to the client's industry, with 3-5 actionable detection rules.

The barrier to entry is low: MISP is free, open feeds are free, and you just need the analytical skill to separate relevant intel from noise. This is a service you can build while still in college and scale as a side business.
