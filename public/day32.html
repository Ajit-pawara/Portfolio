Social Engineering & Phishing

The weakest link in any security chain is the human. Today you understand the psychology, build a phishing simulation, and learn to spot red flags.

THEORY

Why social engineering works: Social engineering exploits human psychology — trust, authority, urgency, fear, greed, and reciprocity — rather than technical vulnerabilities. The most sophisticated technical defenses (firewalls, EDR, MFA) are irrelevant when an attacker can call an employee and convince them to reveal their password. Social engineering is the initial access vector for over 70% of breaches (Verizon DBIR).

Social engineering techniques:

# Phishing — fraudulent emails that appear legitimate, tricking victims into # clicking malicious links, opening malicious attachments, or revealing credentials. # Spear phishing — targeted at a specific individual with personalization. # Whaling — targeting C-level executives with high-value access. # Pretexting — creating a fabricated scenario to obtain information. # "Hi, this is IT support. We're doing a security update and need your password." # Baiting — offering something enticing (free USB drive, download) that contains malware. # Tailgating — following an authorized person through a secured door without credentials. # Vishing (voice phishing) — phone calls impersonating tech support, banks, or vendors. # SMiShing (SMS phishing) — text messages with malicious links.

Email header analysis — detecting spoofing:

# View raw email headers to detect phishing: Received: from mail.evil.com (192.168.1.100) Return-Path: <ceo@company.com> <-- check this From: "CEO" <ceo@compny.com> <-- misspelled domain! Reply-To: attacker@evil.com SPF: fail (sender IP not authorized) DKIM: fail (signature invalid) DMARC: fail (alignment check failed) # Check SPF/DKIM/DMARC with dig: dig txt company.com | grep spf dig txt _dmarc.company.com
The MFA bypass reality: Even multi-factor authentication doesn't stop phishing. Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM) phishing kits relay the victim's session in real-time — the victim enters their password + MFA code, and the attacker uses both instantly. MFA is not a magic bullet against social engineering.
// attacker vs defender mindset
⚔ Attacker
  • Research the target on LinkedIn, company website, and news — find names, roles, projects, and vendors. Use this intel to personalize spear-phishing emails.
  • Register a lookalike domain (e.g., your-company-secure.com vs your-company.com) with a valid SSL cert. The padlock icon convinces many victims.
  • Set up EvilGinx2 or Modlishka for AiTM phishing — the victim enters credentials and MFA codes into a real-time relay to the legitimate site.
  • Call the help desk impersonating an employee — "I lost my phone and can't access my MFA app" — a well-practiced pretext often bypasses verification processes.
🛡 Defender
  • Implement DMARC, DKIM, and SPF email authentication — these prevent domain spoofing from reaching the inbox entirely.
  • Conduct regular simulated phishing campaigns — GoPhish sends controlled test emails; employees who click are auto-enrolled in training.
  • Establish clear verification procedures — any request for password reset, wire transfer, or sensitive data must be verified through a secondary channel (phone call, in-person).
  • Train employees on red flags: misspellings, mismatched sender names, urgent language, unexpected attachments, and requests to bypass normal procedures.
// real named incident
2013-2015 — GOOGLE & FACEBOOK WIRE FRAUD — $100 MILLION
Phishing invoices impersonating Quanta Computer tricked employees into wiring $100M
Lithuanian hacker Evaldas Rimasauskas executed a sophisticated spear-phishing campaign against Google and Facebook. He sent fraudulent invoices that appeared to come from Quanta Computer, a legitimate Taiwanese hardware supplier that both companies did business with. The invoices had realistic-looking letterhead, payment terms, and bank account details — the only difference was the bank account number, which was controlled by Rimasauskas. Employees at both companies processed the payments, wiring over $100 million to the attacker's accounts over two years. The scheme was only discovered when a vigilant employee noticed the bank account didn't match previous invoices. Facebook and Google eventually recovered most of the funds, but the case remains the textbook example of Business Email Compromise (BEC) at scale.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: set up a simulated phishing campaign, analyze email headers for spoofing, and build a pretext.

  1. Install GoPhish: sudo apt install golang-go -y (or use Docker: docker run -d -p 3333:3333 -p 80:80 gophish/gophish). Access the admin UI at https://localhost:3333.
  2. Create a phishing template that mimics a password reset email from a well-known service. Include a link to a landing page that captures credentials.
  3. Send the campaign to a test email address you control. Observe the email in the receiving inbox — check if it landed in Spam (SPF/DKIM failure).
  4. Analyze the email headers: view raw headers in Gmail/Outlook. Identify the original sender IP, SPF/DKIM/DMARC results, and Return-Path.
  5. Use dig txt _dmarc.company.com to check if the target domain has DMARC configured. Note whether it's set to p=none, p=quarantine, or p=reject.
  6. Build a pretext for a phone-based vishing call — a scenario where you call the IT help desk to request a password reset for "your" account.
Deliverable: A GoPhish campaign report showing sent/opened/clicked stats for a simulated phishing email, plus raw email header analysis showing SPF/DKIM results.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "What's the difference between phishing, spear-phishing, and whaling?"
A: "Phishing is a mass, untargeted email sent to many recipients hoping someone clicks. Spear-phishing is targeted at a specific individual or organization using personalized information (name, role, recent activity) to increase credibility. Whaling targets senior executives (CEO, CFO, CTO) who have access to high-value systems and financial authority. Whaling emails are often crafted as legal subpoenas, audit notices, or urgent financial requests."
Q: "How do SPF, DKIM, and DMARC prevent email spoofing?"
A: "SPF (Sender Policy Framework) lists which IP addresses are authorized to send mail for a domain. DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) cryptographically signs emails so the receiver can verify the email wasn't tampered with. DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance) tells receivers what to do if SPF or DKIM fails — p=none (do nothing, just report), p=quarantine (send to spam), or p=reject (block entirely). All three must be configured together to prevent domain spoofing."
Q: "How would you respond if an employee reports clicking a phishing link?"
A: "First, have the employee disconnect from the network immediately. Then: isolate the affected system, force a password reset, invalidate all active sessions, check for installed malware or persistence mechanisms, review email forwarding rules, and analyze the phishing email itself (headers, links, attachments) for IoCs. Escalate to incident response and report to relevant teams. Document everything for post-incident review and training improvement."
AI TOOLS
Phishing email template generator
Describe the pretext and target persona — an LLM can generate a realistic phishing email template with proper formatting, urgency cues, and social engineering triggers.
BEC detection assistant
Paste a suspicious email into an LLM and ask for analysis — it will identify red flags: mismatched sender name, urgency language, unusual reply-to, and grammatical deviations.
Pretext builder
Describe a target (role, company, department) — an LLM can generate a convincing phone pretext: "You're calling from IT support about a security incident on their account."
DMARC report analyzer
Paste a DMARC aggregate report (XML) into an LLM and ask for a summary — which IPs are passing/failing, which sources are unauthorized, and recommended actions.
