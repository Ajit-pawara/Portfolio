Intro to Bug Bounty Hunting

Bug bounty programs pay for findings. Today you learn how to responsibly disclose vulnerabilities, choose programs, and write your first bug bounty report.

THEORY

What is bug bounty hunting? Bug bounty programs are offered by organizations that invite security researchers to find and report vulnerabilities in their systems, applications, and infrastructure in exchange for monetary rewards (bounties). Unlike traditional pentesting where you're hired by a specific client with a defined scope, bug bounty hunting lets you choose your targets, work on your schedule, and get paid per finding. The top bounty hunters earn $1M+ annually, but most hunters earn $5K-50K/year as a side income.

Major bug bounty platforms:

HackerOne
Largest platform with programs from Google, Twitter, Shopify, Nintendo, and 2,000+ others. Hackers have earned over $100M total. Top earners make $1M+.
Bugcrowd
Second largest platform. Features include VRT (Vulnerability Rating Taxonomy) for consistent payout expectations. Programs include Atlassian, Tesla, Spotify.
Intigriti
European platform with strong community focus. Good for non-US researchers. Features a monthly XSS challenge.
Open Bug Bounty
Free, non-monetary platform where you can disclose vulnerabilities to organizations without a formal program. Good for building a reputation portfolio.

Responsible disclosure process:

1. Identify vulnerability 2. Verify it's reproducible and document with screenshots + proof of concept 3. Check program scope — is the target in scope? Is testing permitted? 4. Submit report through the platform (or security@ email for non-platform programs) 5. Include: target, vulnerability type, impact, steps to reproduce, PoC, suggested fix 6. Wait for triage — the team verifies and assigns severity/criticality 7. Coordinate disclosure timeline — typically 30-90 days for remediation 8. Get paid — bounties range from $50 (low severity) to $10,000+ (critical)
Low-hanging fruit for beginners: Missing security headers (X-Frame-Options, CSP, HSTS), exposed .git/config files, CORS misconfigurations, subdomain takeover, open S3 buckets, directory listing enabled, verbose error messages, default credentials, and clickjacking. These won't pay $10K but they build your report-writing skills.
// attacker vs defender mindset
⚔ Attacker
  • Start with recon — subdomain enumeration (subfinder), URL gathering (gau/waybackurls), technology detection (whatweb/wappalyzer), and port scanning. More recon = more attack surface.
  • Prioritize bugs with clear business impact — an IDOR that leaks other users' private data pays more than a missing header. Impact drives bounty amounts.
  • Automate the boring parts — use nuclei for template-based scanning, httpx for probing, and paramspider for parameter discovery. Manual testing on the interesting findings.
🛡 Defender
  • Bug bounty hunters are your allies — a good program invites responsible disclosure instead of forcing attackers to find vulnerabilities through other means.
  • Have a clear scope document — what's in scope, what's out of scope, what's prohibited (no social engineering, no DoS, no physical access). Ambiguous scope leads to wasted researcher effort.
  • Respond to reports quickly — researchers who wait weeks for a response will lose interest and may disclose publicly before you've patched.

HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: Sign up on a bug bounty platform, recon a target, find a low-hanging vulnerability, and write your first professional vulnerability report.

  1. Sign up on HackerOne and/or Bugcrowd. Browse public programs and note their scope, payout ranges, and out-of-scope items.
  2. Choose an in-scope target from a public program with a friendly scope (e.g., a security header audit target). Do NOT test on out-of-scope targets — it's illegal.
  3. Run recon: subfinder -d target.com | httpx -status-code -title to discover subdomains. Then gau target.com to gather URLs.
  4. Check for low-hanging vulnerabilities: security headers (curl -sI https://target.com | grep -i "Strict-Transport-Security\|Content-Security-Policy"), exposed .git (curl https://target.com/.git/config), CORS misconfiguration (curl -H "Origin: https://evil.com" -I https://target.com).
  5. Write a professional vulnerability report following the HackerOne submission template: title, vulnerability type, severity, target, impact, steps to reproduce, screenshots/PoC, affected users, suggested remediation.
  6. If you find a valid vulnerability on a public program in scope, submit it! If not, create a "mock report" for a vulnerability you found in Juice Shop or another lab target for your portfolio.
Deliverable: A professional bug bounty report (real or mock) following the HackerOne submission format. Include: title, severity, impact, steps to reproduce, screenshots, and remediation suggestion. This is the proof that you understand responsible disclosure.

HIGH severity # Check for CORS misconfiguration curl -s -D - -H "Origin: https://evil.com" \ https://target.com/api/endpoint 2>/dev/null | grep -i "access-control" # If Access-Control-Allow-Origin: https://evil.com → misconfigured # Use nuclei for template-based scanning nuclei -u https://target.com -t ~/nuclei-templates/ \ -severity low,medium -silent 2>/dev/null
INTERVIEW READY
Q: "What's your approach to finding vulnerabilities on a new target?"
A: "I start with reconnaissance — subdomain enumeration (subfinder, amass), URL discovery (gau, waybackurls), and technology identification (whatweb, wappalyzer). Then I prioritize testing: first low-hanging fruit like security headers, exposed .git, CORS misconfigurations. Then I test the application functionality — authentication, authorization (IDOR), user input handling (XSS, SQLi), and file upload. I use nuclei for template-based scanning and Burp Suite for manual testing of interesting endpoints. I document everything as I go, so if I find a vulnerability, I can write the report immediately."
Q: "What's the difference between responsible disclosure and full disclosure?"
A: "Responsible disclosure (also called coordinated disclosure) means reporting the vulnerability to the organization privately, giving them time to patch before any public announcement. Full disclosure means publishing the vulnerability details publicly immediately or after a short deadline. Responsible disclosure is the ethical standard — it protects users by giving the vendor time to fix the issue. Full disclosure can pressure vendors who refuse to patch but can also put users at risk if attackers use the public information before a fix is available."
Q: "How would you write a good bug bounty report?"
A: "A good report has 8 elements: a clear title (e.g., 'IDOR in /api/v1/users allows enumeration of all customer PII'), vulnerability type, severity rating (based on CVSS or platform VRT), affected endpoint/component, business impact, step-by-step reproduction instructions with commands and expected vs actual results, proof of concept (screenshot, video, or working exploit), and a suggested remediation. The goal is to make it as easy as possible for the triage team to verify your finding — if they can reproduce it in under 5 minutes, you'll get paid faster."

ENTREPRENEUR ANGLE

Bug bounty experience directly translates into pentesting income. A single critical vulnerability finding on your resume can justify charging $5,000+/day as a freelance pentester. Many companies hire bug bounty hunters directly for private pentesting engagements at premium rates because they've proven their skills through public findings.

Alternative business model: "Vulnerability Disclosure Program Setup" — many organizations want a bug bounty program but don't know how to start one. You can help them define scope, set up a HackerOne/Bugcrowd page, create a disclosure policy, and build a triage workflow. Charge $3,000-8,000 for the setup. You're not just hunting bugs — you're helping companies build the infrastructure to receive bug reports securely and efficiently.
