Writing a Security-Focused Resume

Generic resumes don't get past security hiring managers. Today you tailor your resume with security-specific keywords, quantify achievements, and highlight hands-on lab experience.

THEORY

The security resume is different: Generic resumes list responsibilities. Security resumes list capabilities with measurable outcomes. Hiring managers for SOC analyst, pentester, and AppSec roles look for specific keywords, hands-on tool experience, and evidence of practical application — not just theoretical knowledge. Your resume must pass the Applicant Tracking System (ATS) scan before a human ever sees it.

ATS keywords for security roles (must include):

TOOLS: SIEM, Splunk, Wireshark, Nmap, Metasploit, Burp Suite, Nessus/OpenVAS, Snort/Suricata, EDR, MISP, VirusTotal, YARA, Autopsy, Volatility, Ghidra, Python, Bash, PowerShell, Docker, Git, AWS/Azure/GCP (if cloud path) CONCEPTS: Incident Response, Threat Hunting, Malware Analysis, Digital Forensics, Vulnerability Assessment, Penetration Testing, Risk Management, MITRE ATT&CK, Cyber Kill Chain, OWASP Top 10, Zero Trust, Defense-in-Depth, Least Privilege, IAM, PKI, SIEM Correlation CERTIFICATIONS (if held): CompTIA Security+, CySA+, CEH, OSCP, AWS Security, CCSP

Bullet point transformation — weak vs strong:

❌ Weak (generic, no metrics, no tools)
• Responsible for monitoring network security
• Helped with incident response
• Used security tools to find vulnerabilities
✅ Strong (specific tools, quantified outcomes, active language)
• Configured Splunk SIEM with custom correlation rules, reducing mean detection time for port scans and brute-force attacks by automating alert triage.
• Analyzed 500+ PCAP files using Wireshark and Zeek, identifying C2 beaconing patterns and generating 15+ actionable threat intelligence IoCs.
• Conducted vulnerability assessments with Nessus and OpenVAS across 50+ hosts, prioritizing remediation for 120+ findings based on CVSS scoring.
• Built a home SOC lab with Splunk, Suricata, and Kali Linux, simulating real-world attack scenarios and documenting detection workflows.

Resume structure for entry-level security:

1. NAME + CONTACT + GITHUB/LINKEDIN (top — no summary needed for entry-level) 2. TECHNICAL SKILLS (tools, programming, frameworks — grouped by category) 3. CERTIFICATIONS (if held, with dates) 4. HANDS-ON PROJECTS / LABS (2-3 bullet points each, with tools and outcomes) 5. WORK EXPERIENCE (if any — focus on security-adjacent duties) 6. EDUCATION (degree + relevant coursework) 7. CTF / BUG BOUNTY / COMPETITIONS (if applicable)
ATS tip: Use standard section headings (not creative ones). Don't use columns, tables, graphics, or unusual fonts — ATS parsers break on them. Save your resume as PDF (not Word) but verify the PDF text is selectable — if it's an image-based PDF, ATS reads nothing.
// attacker vs defender mindset
⚔ Attacker
  • Your resume is competing against hundreds of others. The ATS is your first adversary — if your resume doesn't contain the right keywords, it's rejected without a human ever seeing it.
  • Hiring managers spend 6-10 seconds scanning each resume. Make every word count — lead with impact, use active verbs (Configured, Analyzed, Built, Automated, Developed).
  • Tailor your resume for each application — job descriptions contain the keywords you need. If the job says "Splunk" and your resume says "log analysis," you'll lose to someone who said "Splunk."
🛡 Defender
  • Your resume is a security document — don't include personal information beyond what's needed (no photo, no age, no marital status — in many places it's illegal to ask).
  • Don't exaggerate or lie — security hiring managers often verify claims with technical questions. "Proficient in Splunk" means you can write complex SPL queries under interview pressure.
  • Quantify everything — "Analyzed 500 PCAPs" is verifiable if you can describe the analysis in an interview. "Configured Splunk" is verifiable if you can build a dashboard on the spot.

2024 — ATS KEYWORDS FOR SECURITY ROLES — WHAT HIRING SYSTEMS SCAN FOR
ATS systems for security roles scan for: SIEM, Splunk, Wireshark, Nmap, Metasploit, Burp Suite, Python, Bash, incident response, penetration testing, OWASP, MITRE ATT&CK
Analysis of 500+ SOC analyst job descriptions in 2024 revealed the most common ATS keywords: SIEM (85% of postings), incident response (78%), Splunk (62%), Python (58%), Wireshark (55%), Nmap (52%), threat intelligence (48%), vulnerability management (45%), MITRE ATT&CK (42%), and security tools (40%). The key insight: if your resume doesn't contain these exact terms, the ATS filters you out before a recruiter ever opens it. Even if you have equivalent experience ("network traffic analysis" instead of "Wireshark"), the ATS doesn't know — it matches exact terms. This is why tailoring your resume to each job description is essential, not optional.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: Create an ATS-optimized, security-focused resume with quantified accomplishments from your 90-day training.

  1. Extract the top 15 keywords from 3 SOC analyst job descriptions on LinkedIn or Indeed. Use an online ATS keyword extractor or paste into an LLM and ask for keyword analysis.
  2. Write 5 resume bullets in the strong format using your actual 90-day lab experience. Each bullet must include: a tool name, an action verb, and a quantified outcome.
  3. Write your "Projects" section — document your home SOC lab, bug bounty report, CTF writeups, or DevSecOps pipeline. Include tools used and measurable results.
  4. Create a "Skills" section organized by category: SIEM & Log Analysis, Vulnerability Assessment, Scripting & Automation, Frameworks & Standards.
  5. Run your resume through an ATS simulator (jobscan.co or similar free tool) — check your match rate against a target job description.
Deliverable: Your final ATS-optimized resume saved as a text-selectable PDF. Minimum requirements: 5 quantified security bullets, skills section with specific tools, projects section with lab names, and 85%+ ATS match rate against a target security job description.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "Walk me through a project on your resume that demonstrates your security skills."
A: "My home SOC lab project demonstrates my core security capabilities. I set up Splunk Free as the SIEM, configured Suricata as an IDS with custom rules, and used Kali Linux to simulate attacks including port scans (nmap), brute force (hydra), and malware beaconing (custom Python script). I configured log forwarding from Suricata and the Windows endpoint to Splunk, created custom dashboards showing detection metrics, and wrote SPL queries for threat hunting. The project documented over 15 hours of hands-on work and resulted in automated detection of 3/3 simulated attack types. The full documentation is on my GitHub."
Q: "You don't have professional security experience. Why should we hire you over someone who does?"
A: "While I don't have professional security experience, I've invested 90+ days in a structured hands-on training program that covers exactly what your SOC team does daily. I've configured a Splunk SIEM, analyzed PCAPs in Wireshark, written Suricata rules, practiced incident response triage, and documented everything on GitHub. I come in with a learning mindset, no bad habits from other environments, and recent hands-on practice with the tools you use. I'm also Security+ certified, which validates my baseline knowledge across all five security domains."
Q: "What's your biggest weakness and how are you working on it?"
A: "My biggest weakness is depth of experience — I've built a broad foundation across offensive and defensive security, but I haven't had the opportunity to specialize deeply in one area yet. I'm addressing this by choosing my specialization path (SOC/blue team) and focusing my remaining lab time on advanced SIEM correlation, threat hunting methodologies, and preparing for the CySA+ certification. I believe a broad foundation followed by deep specialization is a stronger starting point than deep knowledge of one tool with no context of how it fits into the broader security landscape."
