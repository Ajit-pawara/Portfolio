Mock Technical Interview Practical Round

The second interview tests tool skills. Today you perform a timed practical assessment — Nmap scan, SQLi exploitation, Wireshark analysis, and incident triage.

THEORY

The practical interview format: Unlike the concepts round (Day 85), the practical round gives you a target and a time limit. The interviewer watches how you work — not just whether you get the flag, but your methodology, tool proficiency, note-taking discipline, and reaction to roadblocks. Common formats include: a vulnerable VM you must compromise (Metasploitable, HackTheBox), a PCAP file you must analyze for C2 traffic, a SOC ticket you must triage from log data, or a web app you must test against OWASP Top 10 within a time window.

What interviewers evaluate during practical rounds: Company Mandiant (now part of Google Cloud) is known for its 2-hour practical assessment where candidates analyze a real malware sample and write an intrusion analysis report. They evaluate four dimensions: (1) Technical proficiency — can you use the tools without hand-holding? (2) Methodology — do you work systematically or jump around? (3) Documentation — do you take notes and timestamp findings as you go? (4) Communication — can you explain what you're doing while you do it? The most common reason candidates fail isn't lack of skill — it's freezing under time pressure or failing to communicate their thought process.

Key insight: The practical round is the most important because it's the hardest to prepare for by memorization. The only way to prepare is to practice timed assessments repeatedly. Today's lab recreates the structure of a Mandiant-style practical round using free tools and a home lab target. Treat this as a real interview — set a timer, take notes as if they'll be reviewed, and practice thinking aloud.

The four practical tasks:

Task 1 — Nmap & Recon
nmap -sV -sC -p- target Find open ports, services, and OS. Identify at least 3 potential vulnerabilities.
Task 2 — Exploit
Use Metasploit or manual exploit to gain initial access. Document the exploit chain step by step.
Task 3 — Wireshark Analysis
Open a sample PCAP, identify the attacker IP, the technique used, and the data exfiltrated.
Task 4 — Triage & Report
Write a 1-page intrusion analysis report with executive summary, IOC, timeline, and remediation.
// practical assessment mindset
⚔ Candidate Mindset
  • Talk through everything — interviewers can't evaluate your thinking if you're silent. Say "I'm running an SYN scan first because it's faster, then I'll do version detection on discovered ports."
  • Take timestamped notes — every command, every output, every finding. If you get stuck, review your notes. The notes also become your report deliverable at the end.
  • When stuck, try the next thing. Don't spend 15 minutes on one dead end. Move to another service, another port, another approach. Interviewers note adaptability.
  • Time management: allocate your time before starting. 25% recon, 35% exploitation, 20% analysis, 20% reporting. Set a visible timer.
🛡 Interviewer Mindset
  • They don't expect you to compromise everything — they want to see how you handle the target that fights back. Getting stuck and recovering is worth more than a clean compromise.
  • They watch for tool over-reliance — a candidate who only runs sqlmap and can't interpret the output manually scores lower than one who runs a manual injection first, then uses sqlmap.
  • They check for OPSEC awareness — if you run dangerous exploits without understanding what they do, that's a red flag even if you get root.
  • Report quality is often the deciding factor between two candidates with similar technical performance. Clean, structured documentation beats messy but complete exploitation.

MANDIANT (GOOGLE CLOUD) PRACTICAL INTERVIEW — 2-HOUR INTRUSION ANALYSIS
Mandiant's practical round requires analyzing a real malware sample and writing an intrusion analysis report within 2 hours
Mandiant's practical interview has become a benchmark in the industry. Candidates receive a PCAP file and a malware sample (often based on real APT campaigns). They must determine the initial infection vector, identify C2 infrastructure from PCAP analysis, extract IOCs (IPs, domains, registry keys), understand the malware's capabilities, and write a structured intrusion analysis report. The evaluation focuses on methodology: did they check VirusTotal? Did they use YARA rules? Did they run the malware in a sandbox? Did they timestamp everything? Mandiant reports that fewer than 30% of candidates pass this round on the first attempt, with the most common failure being incomplete documentation rather than technical inability.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: Complete a timed 2-hour practical assessment against Metasploitable 2 with Wireshark PCAP analysis and incident report writing.

  1. Start Metasploitable 2 (VM or Docker: docker run -d tleemcjr/metasploitable2) and identify its IP on your network. Set a 2-hour timer.
  2. Run Nmap recon: nmap -sV -sC -p- [target_ip]. Document all open ports, services, versions, and at least 3 potential vulnerabilities based on version numbers.
  3. Exploit at least one service: try the vsftpd 2.3.4 backdoor (port 21), or the UnrealIRCd backdoor (port 6667), or the Tomcat manager default creds (port 8180). Document your full exploit chain with commands and output.
  4. Open this sample PCAP (download from wget https://wiki.wireshark.org/SampleCaptures/attack.pcap) in Wireshark. Identify the attacker IP, the attack type, and the exfiltrated data.
  5. Write a 1-page intrusion analysis report with: Executive Summary, Timeline of Events, IOCs (IPs, ports, signatures), Indicators of Compromise, Exploit Chain, and Remediation Recommendations.
Deliverable: Your timestamped command log from the entire 2-hour session. A 1-page intrusion analysis report in PDF or Markdown. Screenshot of the compromised machine or Wireshark findings. A 2-minute video or written self-assessment of what you did well and what you'd improve.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "Walk me through how you'd approach a practical penetration test against an unknown target with a 2-hour time limit."
A: "First, I allocate my time — 30 min recon, 45 min exploitation, 30 min analysis, 15 min reporting. I start with an Nmap full port scan and service version detection in parallel. While that runs, I check if there's a web server and run a quick directory scan. I prioritize low-hanging fruit — default credentials, known vulnerable versions (vsftpd, UnrealIRCd), and unauthenticated services. I timestamp and document every command so my report writes itself. If one approach fails after 15 minutes, I pivot to another service."
Q: "You find an open port with an unknown service running. What do you do?"
A: "I use netcat or telnet to connect and read the banner: nc -v [IP] [port]. If that returns nothing useful, I capture traffic to it with tcpdump while I interact, then analyze the protocol format. I search for the banner string in Exploit-DB or searchsploit. I also try common protocol defaults — HTTP methods, SSH identification, FTP anonymous login. If it's truly unknown, I write a Python script to interact with it systematically and document the behavior."
Q: "You just got a root shell on a target during a practical test. What next?"
A: "First, I collect evidence: hostname, IP config, logged-in users, running processes, listening ports, and key files (/etc/passwd, web configs, .bash_history). I take screenshots with timestamps. I attempt to retrieve the flag or sensitive data specified in the test scope. I check for persistence mechanisms. Then I document the full exploit chain from initial foothold to privilege escalation with command outputs. I do NOT run cleanup or destructive actions unless instructed."

ENTREPRENEUR ANGLE

The practical security assessment is a service you can offer directly — small businesses and startups need their web apps and networks tested but can't afford $10,000+ pentest firms. A single-page web app pentest with a 4-hour scope can be delivered for $500-1,000. The template you built today — Nmap recon, service exploitation, Wireshark analysis, structured reporting — is exactly the workflow you'd use for a paid engagement.

Pair this with a "Security Assessment Starter Pack" — a Notion or Google Docs template that includes a methodology checklist, report template, and common exploit commands. Sell it for $25-50 on Gumroad. You've already done the work; packaging it for others is pure margin.

TEACH IT BACK

Explain out loud, in under 90 seconds, without notes: "What's your methodology for a 2-hour practical pentest assessment — walk through the phases and how you allocate time." If you can describe your recon-exploit-analyze-report pipeline confidently, you're ready for the practical round of any senior-level internship or entry-level pentest interview.
