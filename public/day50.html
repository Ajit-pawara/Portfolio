Writing Your First Pentest Report

An undelivered exploit is just a proof of concept. A good pentest report is what gets findings fixed. Today you write a professional-grade report.

THEORY

Why the report matters more than the hack: In a professional penetration test, the actual exploitation is 20% of the work. The other 80% is communication — scoping, debriefs, and the written report. Your client is paying for a clear answer to one question: "What do I need to fix, in what order, and how?" A technically brilliant exploit that's documented in an incomprehensible report is worthless to the client. Conversely, a well-structured report with clear findings and actionable remediation will get you rehired even if the technical depth was moderate.

The standard pentest report structure (PTES):

1. Executive Summary — Non-technical overview for management (1-2 pages) — Risk rating, attack narrative, top 3 findings 2. Scope & Methodology — Test boundaries, in-scope hosts, exclusions — Tools used, testing approach, time window 3. Findings (risk-ranked) — Each finding: Title, CVSS score, Description — Proof of Concept (screenshots, commands) — Remediation (specific, actionable steps) 4. Technical Details — Full commands, output, network maps 5. Miscellaneous — Informational observations, system inventory 6. Appendices — Raw tool output, password lists discovered, references

CVSS scoring for findings: Every finding should include a CVSS v3.1 vector string and base score. The vector encodes: Attack Vector (AV:N for network, AV:L for local), Attack Complexity (AC:L for low), Privileges Required (PR:N for none), User Interaction (UI:N for none), and Impact metrics (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability). Example: A SQL injection that requires no auth and exposes the full database would be CVSS:3.1/AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N/S:U/C:H/I:H/A:H — Base Score 9.8 (Critical). The CVSS score drives the client's remediation priority — if you score it wrong, they may deprioritize a critical finding.

Key insight: Never report a finding without a specific, actionable remediation. "Upgrade the software" is not actionable. "Upgrade Apache from 2.4.41 to 2.4.54 by running 'apt update && apt upgrade apache2' then restart the service" is actionable. Developers and sysadmins should be able to fix the issue directly from your report without needing to ask you what you meant.
// attacker vs defender mindset
⚔ Attacker
  • Think like an attacker when describing impact — "An unauthenticated attacker could extract all user credentials from the database, leading to account takeover and lateral movement." This makes the risk concrete, not abstract.
  • Document every step of the exploitation chain — if your report only shows the final shell, the client can't understand the path you took or where to prioritize defensive spending.
  • Include screenshots with timestamps, commands with output, and exact payloads used. Reproducibility is the standard — the client's team should be able to verify the finding by following your instructions.
  • Be honest about failed attempts — documenting which avenues you tried and why they failed demonstrates thoroughness and saves the client's team from wasting time on the same dead ends.
🛡 Defender
  • Write findings from the defender's perspective — for each vulnerability, explain what the defender should monitor to detect exploitation attempts (WAF logs, SIEM rules, endpoint events).
  • Provide a "remediation roadmap" — group findings by effort (quick wins vs long-term projects) so the client can plan sprint-based remediation.
  • Include a "false positive" section — if you tested something that looked vulnerable but wasn't, note it. This saves the client from investigating known non-issues.
  • Offer retesting terms — specify that you'll verify fixes within 30 days for one round of retesting, and explain how to schedule it.

INDUSTRY STANDARD — PTES & OWASP TESTING GUIDE
The Penetration Testing Execution Standard (PTES) and OWASP Testing Guide provide globally recognized frameworks
PTES was developed in 2009 by a consortium of penetration testing experts to address the lack of standardization in the industry. Before PTES, every pentest firm had their own methodology and report format — clients couldn't compare reports between vendors. PTES defines seven phases: Pre-engagement Interactions, Intelligence Gathering, Threat Modeling, Vulnerability Analysis, Exploitation, Post-Exploitation, and Reporting. The OWASP Testing Guide complements PTES with web-application-specific test cases. Together, they form the de facto standard for professional penetration testing. Following PTES in your reports demonstrates to employers and clients that you understand industry standards, not just hacking techniques.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: write a complete penetration test report based on a vulnerability you've discovered in any previous lab (DVWA, Juice Shop, HTB, or THM).

  1. Choose a finding from a previous lab day — a SQL injection, XSS, or privilege escalation you successfully performed. Re-run it and capture fresh screenshots with timestamps.
  2. Create the report skeleton in Markdown or a Word template: Executive Summary (1 paragraph for management), Scope (the lab environment), and Methodology (tools used).
  3. For the finding section: write a finding title (e.g., "SQL Injection in User ID Parameter — 9.8 Critical"), a CVSS vector and score, and a 3-sentence description of the vulnerability.
  4. Add Proof of Concept: screenshots of the vulnerable request, the exploit payload, and the successful extraction of data. Include exact curl commands or payload strings.
  5. Write the remediation: specific step-by-step instructions. For SQLi: "Replace string concatenation with parameterized queries. In PHP: use PDO prepared statements with bindParam()."
  6. Include a "Risk & Business Impact" section: "If exploited, an unauthenticated attacker could extract the full user database (50K records), leading to GDPR fines up to €20M."
Deliverable: A PDF or Markdown document that follows the PTES report structure with at least one complete finding (executive summary, scope, finding with CVSS, PoC, remediation). This is your portfolio's crown jewel — a work-sample that proves you can communicate security findings professionally.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "What sections does a professional penetration test report contain?"
A: "A professional pentest report follows the PTES standard: Executive Summary (non-technical overview for management), Scope and Methodology (what was tested and how), Findings ranked by severity (each with title, CVSS vector, description, proof of concept, and remediation), Technical Details (raw commands and output), and Appendices. The most important part is the Executive Summary — it's often the only thing the C-suite reads, and it determines whether they take the findings seriously and authorize the budget to fix them."
Q: "How do you write a finding that a developer can actually fix?"
A: "I structure each finding with: a clear description that explains what the vulnerability is and why it's dangerous, a proof of concept that's reproducible (screenshots, exact curl commands, or Burp requests), and a specific remediation that includes code examples. I avoid vague recommendations like 'sanitize input' — instead I say 'replace string concatenation in UserController.php line 45 with parameterized queries using PDO prepared statements' and include a code snippet. The remediation should be a merge-request-ready change."
Q: "How do you handle a situation where the client disagrees with your risk rating?"
A: "I show my CVSS vector and explain each component — Attack Vector, Complexity, Privileges Required, User Interaction, and Impact — so we're discussing the objective standard, not opinions. I also provide context: 'This finding is Critical because it requires no authentication, no user interaction, and results in complete confidentiality loss.' If the client still disagrees, I help them understand that accepting a risk is a business decision they can make, but I'm obligated to report it at the correct severity according to industry standards."

ENTREPRENEUR ANGLE

Report writing is the most underrated skill in freelancing. Most technical pentesters are great at exploitation but terrible at documentation. If you can write a clear, professional report, you can out-earn more technically skilled testers simply because clients re-hire the person who communicates well. A well-written report justifies the $10,000-50,000 engagement cost — a poorly written one generates scope creep, clarification calls, and unhappy clients.

Productized opportunity: "Report-as-a-Service" for freelance pentesters. Many solo testers don't want to spend 3 days writing a report after a 2-day test. You offer to take their raw notes, screenshots, and findings and produce a client-ready report in 24 hours. Charge $500-1,500 per report. The pentester gets to focus on testing; you get paid for writing. Build templates for different standards (PTES, OWASP, PCI) and you can scale this to 3-4 reports per week.
