Security Policies & Compliance

Security is as much about policy as it is about technology. Today you understand ISO 27001 ISMS, GDPR data protection principles, and how compliance drives security spending.

THEORY

What is an ISMS? An Information Security Management System (ISMS) is a systematic framework for managing sensitive information. ISO 27001 is the international standard for ISMS — it specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an organization's security posture. Unlike a technology checklist, ISO 27001 is risk-based: you assess your specific risks and implement controls (from Annex A's 93 controls across 14 domains) that are appropriate to those risks.

Key compliance frameworks compared:

ISO 27001
International ISMS standard. 93 controls across 14 domains. Certification required. Annual audits. Risk-based approach.
GDPR
EU data protection regulation. Applies to any org processing EU citizen data. Fines up to 4% of global revenue or 20M. Data subject rights: access, rectification, erasure, portability.
NIST CSF
US framework — Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. Voluntary but widely adopted. Less prescriptive than ISO 27001.
PCI DSS
Payment Card Industry standard. 12 requirements for handling credit card data. Required for any business processing cards.

GDPR Data Subject Rights and Breach Notification: GDPR grants individuals eight rights including the right to be informed, right of access, right to rectification, right to erasure (right to be forgotten), and right to data portability. Crucially for security professionals: GDPR Article 33 requires breach notification to the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data breach. Article 34 requires notification to affected data subjects if the breach poses a high risk to their rights and freedoms. This means every incident responder must know how to determine whether a breach involves personal data and calculate the 72-hour notification window.

Why this matters: Compliance is often the primary driver of security budgets. A CISO justifying a SIEM purchase to the board will say "ISO 27001 control A.12.6.1 requires us to monitor and log security events" — not "we should buy a SIEM." Understanding compliance frameworks is how you translate technical security needs into business language that decision-makers understand.
// attacker vs defender mindset
ATTACKER
  • Target organizations during compliance audit periods when security teams are distracted by paperwork and evidence gathering rather than threat monitoring.
  • Exploit the gap between compliance (checking boxes) and actual security — many orgs pass audits but have weak real-world defenses because they focus on documentation over capability.
  • Use GDPR as a weapon — report a company to the DPA for a minor incident, forcing a regulatory investigation that distracts their security team while you continue operations.
  • Focus on data subjects' personal data — GDPR creates a legal penalty for breach of personal data. The higher the fine potential, the more leverage the attacker has in ransom negotiations.
DEFENDER
  • Map every security control to a compliance requirement — this justifies tooling and staffing costs to management. "We need EDR because it addresses ISO 27001 A.12.4.1 (event logging)."
  • Create a compliance-to-technical-control spreadsheet: for each ISO 27001 control, document the specific technical implementation (SIEM rule, firewall policy, access review schedule).
  • Practice the 72-hour breach notification drill — time how long it takes from detection to determining if personal data was involved. Most organizations fail this timeline.
  • Use GDPR as a security driver — when you need budget for data classification tools or DLP, frame it as "GDPR compliance requirement" which management understands better than "data security."
// real named incident
2023 — META — €1.2B GDPR FINE
Meta fined €1.2B by the Irish DPC for violating GDPR data transfer requirements — the largest GDPR fine in history
In May 2023, Meta was fined €1.2 billion by the Irish Data Protection Commission (DPC) for transferring personal data of EU users to the United States in violation of GDPR Article 45-49 (international data transfer requirements). The fine, the largest in GDPR history, represented 4% of Meta's 2022 global revenue — the maximum possible penalty. The case highlighted that compliance violations do not require a security breach — simply failing to meet data protection requirements is enough. Meta was ordered to suspend data transfers to the US within 5 months and delete unlawfully transferred data. The ruling forced Meta to re-architect its data infrastructure, a cost estimated at $500M+. This case demonstrates that compliance failures can be as financially damaging as security breaches.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: create a compliance checklist mapping ISO 27001 Annex A controls to specific technical implementations, and simulate a GDPR breach notification.

  1. Download the ISO 27001:2022 Annex A control list (available free from ISO or via summary documents online). Focus on Domain A.12 (Operations Security) and A.8 (Asset Management).
  2. Create a spreadsheet with columns: Control ID, Control Name, Technical Implementation, Responsible Team, Status (Implemented/Partial/Missing).
  3. Map 5 controls to real technical implementations. Example: A.12.6.1 (Vulnerability Management) → "Weekly OpenVAS scans on all internet-facing systems. Critical vulns patched within 48 hours."
  4. For the GDPR drill: write a simulated breach scenario — "An employee laptop with 5,000 customer records (names + emails) was stolen from a coffee shop." Determine: does this trigger Article 33 notification? What's the risk to data subjects? Draft the notification email to the DPA.
  5. Calculate the 72-hour window: if the theft was discovered at 10:00 AM on Monday, what is the deadline? When does the clock start? (Answer: when you become aware, not when the incident occurred.)
  6. Document everything in a single-page compliance reference sheet you can use during interviews.
Deliverable: a compliance checklist spreadsheet (5+ controls mapped) AND a simulated GDPR breach notification draft. These demonstrate you can bridge the gap between security operations and regulatory requirements.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "What is ISO 27001 and what is its approach to security?"
A: "ISO 27001 is an international standard for Information Security Management Systems (ISMS). It takes a risk-based approach — organizations assess their specific risks and implement appropriate controls from Annex A (93 controls across 14 domains). It's not a technology checklist; it's a management framework that requires continuous improvement, management review, and internal audits. Certification involves an external audit by an accredited body and requires recertification every three years."
Q: "What happens when a GDPR personal data breach occurs?"
A: "The organization must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach, per Article 33. The notification must describe the nature of the breach, categories and number of data subjects and records, likely consequences, and measures taken. If the breach poses a high risk to data subjects, Article 34 requires direct notification to affected individuals. The organization must also document the breach in an internal breach register including root cause and remedial actions taken."
Q: "How do you demonstrate to management that security spending is justified?"
A: "By mapping security controls to compliance requirements. For example, I'd say 'ISO 27001 control A.12.6.1 requires vulnerability management — we need a scanner. Control A.12.4.1 requires event logging — we need a SIEM.' Management understands compliance mandates because non-compliance means legal liability, fines, and loss of business. I'd also present risk-based numbers: 'A data breach costs $4.45M on average. This EDR tool costs $50K/year. The ROI is clear.' The compliance framework provides the language to translate security needs into business terms."
