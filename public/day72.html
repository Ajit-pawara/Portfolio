Risk Assessment Basics

You can't protect everything equally. Risk assessment helps you decide what to protect first. Today you perform a qualitative risk assessment using NIST frameworks.

THEORY

What is Risk Assessment? Risk assessment is the process of identifying assets, threats, vulnerabilities, and the likelihood and impact of exploitation — then calculating a risk score to prioritize remediation. The formula is simple: Risk = Likelihood × Impact. But the methodology behind it is rigorous. NIST SP 800-30 (Guide for Conducting Risk Assessments) provides the gold-standard framework used across government and private sector.

Qualitative vs Quantitative Risk:

# Qualitative Risk (most common — uses ordinal scales) Risk = Likelihood × Impact Likelihood: Very Low (1) | Low (2) | Moderate (3) | High (4) | Very High (5) Impact: Very Low (1) | Low (2) | Moderate (3) | High (4) | Very High (5) Risk Score: 1-25. Score ≥12 = High Risk (requires immediate action) # Quantitative Risk (uses monetary values — annualized) ALE = SLE × ARO SLE (Single Loss Expectancy): $500,000 (cost of one breach event) ARO (Annual Rate of Occurrence): 0.2 (once every 5 years) ALE (Annual Loss Expectancy): $100,000/year

Inherent vs Residual Risk: Inherent risk is the risk level before any controls are applied. Residual risk is what remains after controls are implemented. The gap between the two represents the effectiveness of your security controls. For example: a web server has HIGH inherent risk (it's internet-facing). After applying a WAF, patching, and access controls, the residual risk may be MODERATE. If residual risk is still above the organization's risk appetite, additional controls are needed. This is the core of risk-based decision-making.

Why this matters: Every security decision — which vulnerability to patch first, which tool to buy, which project to fund — should be driven by risk assessment. Without it, you're guessing. With it, you can defend your priorities to management with data: "We should spend $50K on this because it reduces our residual risk from Critical to Low."
// attacker vs defender mindset
ATTACKER
  • Target assets the defender undervalues in their risk assessment — often backup servers, test environments, and staging systems have lower priority but provide lateral movement paths to crown jewels.
  • Exploit the gap between inherent risk (what the organization knows is dangerous) and actual controls (what they've implemented) — especially for "accepted" risks that were deemed low priority.
  • Focus on threats that appear in the "Moderate" risk band — organizations prioritize Critical and High, leaving Moderate risks under-defended. A chain of Moderate risks can escalate to a Critical breach.
  • Monitor the risk assessment schedule — if assessments are annual, exploit vulnerabilities that emerge mid-cycle before the next assessment identifies them.
DEFENDER
  • Update risk assessments continuously, not annually — every new CVE, every new asset, every network change can alter risk posture. Quarterly reviews with monthly threat updates is a good cadence.
  • Document risk acceptance decisions formally — when management accepts a risk (e.g., "we won't patch this system because it would cause downtime"), get it signed. This protects you and shows due diligence.
  • Use the risk register to justify budget — "We have 12 Critical-risk items. Reducing each to Moderate requires $X." Risk scores speak louder than technical arguments with finance teams.
  • Include third-party risk in assessments — a vendor's breach is your breach. Map each third party's access level and calculate the risk they introduce to your environment.

ONGOING — JPMORGAN CHASE — $600M+ ANNUAL CYBERSPENDING
JPMorgan Chase uses risk assessment frameworks to drive $600M+ annual cybersecurity budget allocation
JPMorgan Chase, the largest US bank by assets, spends over $600 million annually on cybersecurity and employs 3,000+ security staff. Their spending is driven entirely by risk assessment: each business unit undergoes risk assessment to identify crown jewels (payment systems, trading platforms, customer data stores), and the risk scores directly determine budget allocation. After the 2014 breach of 76 million households (due to an unpatched server), JPMorgan overhauled their assessment methodology. Today, they calculate inherent risk for each system using NIST-based scoring, then measure residual risk after controls. Systems with residual risk above the board-approved appetite trigger automatic additional investment. This risk-based approach is why JPMorgan is considered the gold standard for financial sector cybersecurity maturity.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: perform a qualitative risk assessment on a hypothetical (or real) college IT system — identify assets, threats, vulnerabilities, calculate risk scores, and create a treatment plan.

  1. Identify 5 assets in a typical college IT environment: Student Database (crown jewel), Public Website, Email Server, WiFi Network, Lab Computers.
  2. For each asset, identify threats: Student DB → SQL injection, insider threat, ransomware. Public Website → DDoS, defacement. Email Server → phishing, account takeover.
  3. For each asset-threat pair, assign a Likelihood (1-5) and Impact (1-5) score. Calculate Risk = L × I. Use the 5x5 matrix to determine the risk level (Very Low through Critical).
  4. Identify existing controls for each asset: Student DB → WAF, MFA, encryption. Public Website → CDN, patching schedule. Email → SPF, DKIM, DMARC, spam filter.
  5. Calculate residual risk after controls. For the Student DB: inherent risk = 5×5=25 (Critical). After controls (WAF, MFA, encryption), residual risk might be 2×3=6 (Low). Document this gap.
  6. Create a risk treatment plan: for each risk above the acceptable threshold, choose one of four options — Reduce (apply controls), Accept (document and monitor), Transfer (buy cyber insurance), Avoid (remove the system).
Deliverable: a risk assessment matrix with 5 assets, their likelihood/impact scores, inherent and residual risk, and a treatment plan. This is the document format used by every GRC (Governance, Risk, Compliance) team.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "Walk me through how you'd perform a risk assessment for a new web application."
A: "First, identify the asset and its value — a customer-facing web app processing credit cards is high value. Identify threats: SQL injection, XSS, authentication bypass, DDoS. Assign likelihood (how likely is each threat given our current controls?) and impact (financial loss, reputational damage, regulatory fines). Calculate inherent risk using a 5x5 matrix. Assess existing controls: WAF, authentication, encryption, patching schedule. Recalculate residual risk — if it's still above the organization's risk appetite, recommend additional controls. Document everything in a risk register with treatment decisions: reduce, accept, transfer, or avoid."
Q: "What's the difference between inherent risk and residual risk?"
A: "Inherent risk is the risk level assuming no controls are in place — the raw danger of a situation. For example, an internet-facing web server has inherently high risk because it's exposed to the entire internet. Residual risk is what remains after you apply controls — the WAF, patching, access control, and monitoring reduce that risk to a lower level. The gap between inherent and residual measures your control effectiveness. If residual risk is still above the organization's appetite, you need more controls. The goal is to bring residual risk within acceptable tolerance."
Q: "How do you decide which security projects to prioritize when budget is limited?"
A: "I prioritize by residual risk scores. I'd list every risk in the risk register sorted by residual risk (highest first). Projects that address Critical and High residual risks get priority. I calculate the risk reduction per dollar spent — a $10K control that reduces a Critical risk to Low has better ROI than a $50K control that reduces a Moderate risk to Low. I also consider compliance requirements (controls that map to ISO 27001 or regulatory mandates get priority since non-compliance has direct financial penalties). The risk register is the objective tool for these trade-off decisions."
