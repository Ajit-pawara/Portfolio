DAY 87 OF 90 — PHASE 5: CAREER & CAPSTONE

Internship & Entry-Role Hunting Strategy

Finding the first security role is the hardest step. Today you create a job search strategy targeting internships, apprenticeships, and entry-level SOC roles.

THEORY

The cybersecurity job market for entry-level: The 2024 (ISC)² Cybersecurity Workforce Study found a global shortage of 4.8 million cybersecurity professionals. However, the paradox is that many job postings require 2-3 years of experience for "entry-level" roles. The key is knowing where entry-level actually exists: Managed Security Service Providers (MSSPs) like SecureWorks, Trustwave, and Arctic Wolf regularly hire analysts with zero experience because they run tier-1 SOCs at scale. Government programs (NSA Apprenticeship, CISA Cyber Student Program) explicitly target candidates with no experience. Large tech companies run rotational security engineer programs (Microsoft Security Academy, Google SRE residency).

Where entry-level security roles actually are:

MSSP SOC Analysts
SecureWorks, Trustwave, Arctic Wolf, AlertLogic, Expel — hire analysts with minimal experience and train them on their SIEM/EDR stack.
Government Programs
NSA Cyber Apprenticeship, CISA Cyber Student, DoD Cyber Scholarship. 0-2 years experience, clearance sponsorship available.
Big Tech Rotational
Microsoft Security Academy, Google SRE Residency, Amazon Security Engineer Apprenticeship. Competitive but pay + training are unmatched.
Consulting Firms
Deloitte Cyber, PwC Security, EY Cybersecurity — hire junior analysts for compliance, advisory, and light technical work.
Startups
Early-stage security startups often hire generalists who can grow with the company. Less structured but faster skill building.
Local/Regional
Banks, hospitals, universities, and local government. Often overlooked, many have small security teams and are willing to train.
Key insight: The best strategy is "spray and pray for volume, sniper for quality." Apply to 50+ roles broadly (the spray) but also identify 10 dream companies and target them specifically (the sniper). For the sniper roles, find mutual connections on LinkedIn, research the team, and tailor your portfolio to their tech stack. The spray gets you practice with applications and interviews; the sniper gets you the job you want.

Application tracking: The average security job seeker needs 20-40 applications to get one offer. Without a tracking system, you'll lose track of what you applied to, at what stage, with what resume version. Create a spreadsheet with columns: Company, Role, Date Applied, Resume Version, Cover Letter (Y/N), Contact, Status (Applied/Phone/Interview/Rejected/Offer), Notes. This system prevents double-applying, helps you follow up, and identifies patterns in rejections that you can fix.

// job hunting mindset
⚔ Job Seeker Mindset
  • Treat job hunting as a systematic process, not a reaction. Dedicate 1 hour daily to searching, applying, networking, and skill building — just like any other security task.
  • Apply to roles even if you meet only 60% of the requirements. Postings are wish lists, not minimum criteria. The cybersecurity skills gap means employers compromise on qualifications.
  • Follow up on every application after 7 days with a polite LinkedIn message or email. Most applicants ghost; a follow-up puts you in the top 5% of candidates.
  • Create multiple resume versions tailored to different roles: one for SOC analyst (emphasis on monitoring, triage, SIEM), one for pentest (tools, CTFs, labs), one for GRC (frameworks, compliance, policy).
🛡 Hiring Manager Mindset
  • They scan a resume in under 10 seconds. If they can't find your skills, certifications, and relevant experience in that time, you're rejected — even if you're qualified.
  • Cover letters matter for entry-level. A tailored 3-paragraph cover letter referencing the specific company and role shows effort that 95% of applicants don't make.
  • They check your LinkedIn before calling you. If your LinkedIn doesn't match your resume, or if there's no portfolio/learning evidence, you drop below candidates who have them.
  • They value candidates who ask smart questions in interviews: "What does your SIEM stack look like?" "How does the team handle after-hours incidents?" "What's the career progression from Tier 1 SOC to Tier 2?"
// real named incident
NSA CYBERSECURITY APPRENTICESHIP PROGRAM — 100+ ANALYSTS PER YEAR
The NSA's 3-year paid cybersecurity apprenticeship hires 100+ analysts annually with zero experience required
The National Security Agency's (NSA) Cybersecurity Apprenticeship Program (CAP) is one of the most overlooked entry points into security. It requires no prior experience — only a high school diploma, US citizenship, and the ability to obtain a Top Secret clearance. Apprentices are paid from Day 1, receive three years of structured training covering network defense, reverse engineering, forensics, and incident response, and are placed into security roles within the NSA upon completion. The program has a 90%+ retention rate because by the time apprentices finish, they have skills equivalent to analysts with 5+ years of experience. Many similar programs exist at CISA, FBI, and DoD agencies. The lesson: government cyber programs are hiring aggressively and training from scratch.
HANDS-ON LAB

Goal: Build your job application infrastructure — target company list, tailored cover letter templates, and application tracking system.

  1. Create a spreadsheet (Google Sheets, Excel, or Airtable) with columns: Company, Role, Date Applied, Status, Resume Version, Cover Letter, Contact Name, Notes. Save it as "Security Job Tracker."
  2. Research and list 20+ target companies across MSSPs (SecureWorks, Arctic Wolf, Trustwave), government (NSA, CISA, DoD), big tech (Microsoft Security, Google SRE), consulting (Deloitte, PwC, EY), and local/regional firms.
  3. Write one tailored cover letter template with placeholders [COMPANY], [ROLE], [SKILLS] — then customize it for 3 specific applications today.
  4. Search for "SOC Analyst," "Security Analyst," "Cybersecurity Intern," and "Penetration Tester" on LinkedIn, Indeed, and Glassdoor. Apply to at least 5 roles today.
  5. For each application, note the required tools/technologies. If you don't know one (e.g., Splunk, QRadar, CrowdStrike), spend 15 minutes learning the basics and add it to your "in progress" notes.
  6. Set up daily email alerts for 5 job search keywords on LinkedIn, Indeed, and Glassdoor.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "Why do you want to work in cybersecurity, and why at this company specifically?"
A: "I want to work in cybersecurity because I enjoy solving puzzles where the stakes are real — every vulnerability I find represents real risk reduction. I chose to apply to [Company] specifically because I saw that you use [technology] in your SOC stack, which aligns with the skills I've built during my 90-day portfolio. I also read that your team [specific accomplishment or news about the company], and I want to contribute to that mission. I've completed 90 days of structured security training covering network pentesting, SIEM operations, and web app testing, and I'm looking for a place where I can apply that foundation and grow."
Q: "What do you do if you don't hear back from an application?"
A: "I follow up after 7-10 days with a polite email or LinkedIn message expressing continued interest. I keep the message brief — referencing the role I applied for, reiterating my enthusiasm, and offering to answer questions. I find a connection at the company if possible — someone in the security team or a mutual connection — and ask for a 5-minute informational interview to learn more about their work. Even if the original application doesn't work out, the informational interview can open other doors."
Q: "What's more important — certifications or hands-on experience?"
A: "For getting past HR filters, certifications matter more initially. But for passing technical interviews and keeping a job, hands-on experience matters more. My strategy is to pursue both: I'm preparing for the PNPT certification (which is practical, not multiple choice) while building hands-on experience through HackTheBox, TryHackMe, and my 90-day portfolio. The portfolio serves as concrete evidence that I can apply what I've studied — and that's what ultimately convinces hiring managers."
