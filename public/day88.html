Capstone Project Plan Your Pentest Report

Your capstone ties everything together. Today you plan a full penetration test against a home lab target, defining scope, rules of engagement, and testing methodology.

THEORY

Why a capstone pentest report matters more than any lab: Throughout this 90-day journey, you've performed dozens of individual labs — SQL injection here, Nmap scan there, Wireshark analysis somewhere else. A capstone project assembles everything into a single coherent deliverable that mimics what a professional pentester produces. 60% of security hiring managers surveyed say a well-structured penetration test report is more valuable than a certification in evaluating entry-level candidates. Why? Because a lab shows you can follow instructions; a pentest report shows you can plan, execute, document, and communicate — end to end.

The anatomy of a professional pentest report: Every pentest report follows a standard structure regardless of the firm (Rapid7, CrowdStrike, Deloitte, boutique shops). The sections are: (1) Executive Summary — non-technical overview for management, covering what was tested, what was found, overall risk level, and business impact. (2) Scope & Methodology — what was in/out of scope, testing dates, tools used, and approach (blackbox/greybox/whitebox). (3) Risk Ratings — findings sorted by CVSS score with business context. (4) Detailed Findings — each vulnerability with description, proof (screenshots, commands), CVSS vector, and remediation steps. (5) Recon Data — subdomains, IPs, open ports, technologies discovered. (6) Exploit Chains — step-by-step walkthroughs of multi-step compromises. (7) Remediation Appendix — prioritized fix recommendations.

Key insight: Your capstone doesn't need to find a zero-day or compromise a Fortune 500 company. It needs to demonstrate methodology. A clean, well-documented report on a Metasploitable 2 compromise with proper scope, risk ratings, and remediation beats a chaotic report on a real-world pentest with no structure. Hiring managers are evaluating your ability to structure information, not your ability to find rare vulnerabilities.

Rules of Engagement (RoE) document: Before any real pentest starts, the tester and client sign a RoE that defines:

Out of Scope (DO NOT TOUCH)
Production customer database Third-party hosted services HR/Payroll systems Social engineering DoS / DDoS attacks Physical security testing
In Scope (APPROVED)

⚔ Pentester Mindset
  • Plan the attack before touching a keyboard — scope definition, target list, tool selection, exploit prioritization, and contingency plans for when the first approach fails.
  • Document everything as you go — timestamped commands, tool outputs, screenshots with time/date stamps. A professional report is written during the test, not after.
  • Have a fallback for every step. If Nmap SYN scan doesn't work (firewall), use TCP connect scan. If no RCE on port 80, check port 443, then 8080, then enumerate UDP.
  • Know when to stop. If you gain root or domain admin, that's enough for a finding. Don't keep poking — the risk of damage increases with every unnecessary action.
🛡 Client Mindset
  • The client cares about three things: are my systems vulnerable, how bad is it if exploited, and what do I do to fix it? Answer these three questions clearly and you've done your job.
  • They need an Executive Summary that their CISO can forward to the board. Technical detail goes in the appendix. If the executive summary mentions SQL injection, the board won't understand; it needs to say "customer data at risk."
  • False positives waste the client's time. Verify every finding before including it. A report with 3 confirmed critical findings is more valuable than one with 15 unverified alerts.
  • Remediation steps must be actionable. Not "patch everything" but "upgrade Apache from 2.4.49 to 2.4.51 on server web-01 (192.168.1.10) and implement ModSecurity with OWASP CRS."

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "Walk me through the structure of a professional penetration test report."
A: "A professional pentest report starts with an Executive Summary for non-technical stakeholders — what was tested, overall risk level, business impact. Then Scope defines what was in and out of bounds. Methodology describes the testing approach (blackbox, tools used, phases). A Risk Summary table lists findings with CVSS scores. Detailed Findings section has individual writeups for each vulnerability with proof screenshots, CVSS vectors, and remediation steps. If there were multi-step attacks, an Exploit Chains section documents them. Finally, a Remediation Appendix provides prioritized fix instructions."
Q: "What's the difference between a vulnerability scanner finding and a manual pentest finding?"
A: "A scanner finding is automated output — it might say 'TLS 1.0 enabled' with a CVSS score of 3.7. A manual finding includes business context: 'The TLS 1.0 protocol is enabled on the customer-facing login page at login.example.com, allowing a man-in-the-middle attacker to downgrade the connection and intercept credentials. Only 3 of the 10 user sessions observed used TLS 1.2. Remediation: disable TLS 1.0 on the load balancer by [date].' Manual findings include exploitation proof, business impact analysis, and specific remediation steps."
Q: "How do you determine the scope of a penetration test?"
A: "Scope is determined through a kickoff meeting with the client. I ask: what systems are most critical, what's the risk appetite, are there third-party systems or data we should exclude, what time window works for testing, and is this a blackbox (no info), greybox (some credentials), or whitebox (full access) test. I document everything in a Rules of Engagement document that both parties sign before any testing begins. The RoE protects both the client and the tester."
