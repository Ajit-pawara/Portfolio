What is Cybersecurity? + the CIA Triad

Before any tool, any language, any certificate — every security decision traces back to three words. Today you learn them as a working lens, not a flashcard definition.

THEORY

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting three properties of information and systems: that data stays private, stays accurate, and stays available when needed. This is the CIA Triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — and it's the single model every other concept in this roadmap will map onto.

C
Confidentiality
Only the right people can see the data.
I
Integrity
Data isn't altered without authorization.
A
Availability
Systems are usable when legitimately needed.
// real, named incidents — not hypotheticals
2017 — CONFIDENTIALITY BROKEN
Equifax
An unpatched Apache Struts vulnerability let attackers access the personal data of roughly 147 million people — names, SSNs, birth dates. Nothing was altered, nothing went offline. Pure confidentiality failure, and it's still the textbook case study for "patch your stuff."
2010 — INTEGRITY BROKEN
Stuxnet
This worm didn't steal or leak anything — it secretly altered the logic running Iranian centrifuge controllers, making them spin at damaging speeds while reporting normal readings. The data shown to operators was a lie. Classic integrity attack.
2016 — AVAILABILITY BROKEN
Dyn DNS / Mirai Botnet
A botnet of hijacked IoT devices flooded Dyn's DNS servers with traffic, knocking Twitter, Netflix, Reddit, and Spotify offline for hours across the US. No data was stolen or changed — the services simply became unreachable.
// attacker mindset vs. defender mindset
⚔ How an attacker sees CIA
  • "Which of these three is weakest at this target, and cheapest for me to break?"
  • Confidentiality is usually the highest-value target (data sells, or extorts).
  • Availability attacks (DDoS/ransomware) are often the loudest, fastest way to cause pain with the least skill required.
🛡 How a defender sees CIA
  • "Which of these three matters most for THIS specific system, and what's my budget to protect it?"
  • A hospital prioritizes Availability (patient care can't wait) almost as much as Confidentiality.
  • A bank prioritizes Integrity above almost everything — a wrong number is catastrophic even if no one "stole" anything.

INTERVIEW READY
Q: "Walk me through the CIA Triad with a real example."
A: "Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. Equifax in 2017 is a clean Confidentiality failure — 147 million records exposed, nothing altered. Stuxnet is the classic Integrity attack — it fed false sensor data while secretly damaging equipment. And the 2016 Dyn DNS attack, powered by the Mirai botnet, is a textbook Availability failure — Twitter and Netflix went dark for hours with nothing stolen or changed. Every incident I study, I try to place against this triad first — it's the fastest way to understand impact."
ENTREPRENEUR ANGLE

When you eventually pitch a small business on a basic security review, you won't say "I'll hack your stuff" — you'll say "I'll assess your exposure across Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability and hand you a one-page risk summary." That single sentence, framed around a model business owners can actually picture (data privacy, data accuracy, uptime), is the opening line of your future firm's first service offering. Most self-taught beginners never learn to frame their value this clearly — you're building that habit from Day 1.

TEACH IT BACK

Before you post anything publicly, explain the CIA Triad out loud to someone non-technical — a parent, a friend, anyone — using zero jargon. Try this framing: "Imagine your diary. Confidentiality is making sure only you can read it. Integrity is making sure no one secretly rewrites pages in it. Availability is making sure you can actually find and open it when you want to." If you can get a non-technical person to nod and say "okay, that makes sense" — you've actually learned it. If you can't, you've memorized it, not understood it.
