Cryptography III — SSL/TLS Certificates & PKI

The padlock icon in your browser exists because of a complex trust chain involving Certificate Authorities, digital signatures, and PKI. In 2011, DigiNotar — a Dutch CA — was hacked, and a fraudulent Google.com certificate was used to intercept 300,000 Iranian Gmail users. Today you understand exactly how that was possible.

THEORY

The problem certificates solve: when you connect to google.com, your browser verifies the server's public key (from Day 17) — but how does it know the key actually belongs to Google and not an attacker? Anyone could generate an RSA key pair and claim it belongs to Google. Certificates and PKI solve this trust problem.

What is a TLS Certificate? A certificate is a digital document that binds a public key to an identity (domain name, organization). It contains:

Subject
Who this certificate belongs to — the domain name (CN=google.com) or organization. What you're trusting.
Public Key
The server's public key — what your browser uses to establish the encrypted session (Day 17's TLS handshake).
Issuer (CA)
Which Certificate Authority signed this certificate and vouches for it. DigiCert, Let's Encrypt, Comodo are real CAs.
Digital Signature
The CA's cryptographic signature on the certificate — proves the CA verified and approved it. Tamper-evident.
Validity Period
Not Before / Not After dates. An expired certificate triggers browser warnings — attackers often exploit forgotten expired certs.
SAN / CN
Subject Alternative Names — which domain names this certificate covers. Wildcard certs (*.google.com) cover all subdomains.

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) — the trust chain explained:

1
Root CA: the top of the chain — a handful of organizations (DigiCert, Comodo, Let's Encrypt, government CAs) whose root certificates are pre-installed in your OS and browser. You trust them by default.
2
Intermediate CA: Root CAs rarely sign certificates directly — they issue intermediate CA certificates and keep their root key offline in HSMs. Intermediate CAs do the day-to-day signing. This limits damage if an intermediate is compromised.
3
Certificate Signing Request (CSR): a website owner generates a private key + CSR (containing their public key and domain info) and sends the CSR to a CA. The private key never leaves their server.
4
CA verifies ownership (DNS record, file upload, or email) then signs the certificate with its private key — producing the final .crt file the server presents to browsers.
5
Browser validates: on connection, browser checks the certificate's CA signature (using the CA's public key from your trusted store), checks validity dates, checks the domain matches, checks it's not revoked (CRL/OCSP). All pass = padlock shown.
Why the chain matters: if ANY CA in your trusted store is compromised, attackers can issue fraudulent certificates for ANY domain — and browsers will trust them. DigiNotar in 2011 was a trusted CA. When it was breached, the attackers could make a browser believe any server was google.com — and it would show a valid padlock.

Certificate types — know these for client engagements:

DV (Domain Validated)
Only proves you control the domain — no org identity check. Let's Encrypt issues these for free. What most websites use.
OV (Organization Validated)
CA verifies the organization's legal existence. Shows org name in cert details. Good for business sites.
EV (Extended Validation)
Strictest — CA verifies legal identity, physical address, operational existence. Used by banks. Green address bar (legacy browsers).
Self-Signed
No CA involved — you sign your own cert. Free, fast, but browsers show a "Not Secure" warning. Used in internal corporate networks and labs.
Certificate Revocation: if a private key is stolen, the certificate must be revoked. CRL (Certificate Revocation List) and OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol) are the two mechanisms. OCSP stapling is the modern, more efficient approach — the server pre-fetches and caches its own revocation status to serve alongside the certificate.
// real named incident
2011 — DIGINOTAR BREACH — 300,000 IRANIAN GMAIL USERS INTERCEPTED
A trusted Certificate Authority was hacked — attackers issued a fraudulent Google.com certificate and MitM'd hundreds of thousands of users
DigiNotar was a Dutch Certificate Authority trusted by all major browsers and operating systems. In 2011, attackers (widely attributed to Iranian state actors) breached DigiNotar's systems and issued fraudulent TLS certificates for google.com and over 500 other domains including yahoo.com, mozilla.org, microsoft.com, and CIA.gov. Because DigiNotar was in every browser's trusted CA store, these fraudulent certificates showed a valid padlock — browsers had no way to tell they were fake without additional mechanisms. The certificates were used to conduct man-in-the-middle attacks against approximately 300,000 Iranian internet users, intercepting their Gmail communications. The attack was only discovered when a single Iranian user noticed an unusual certificate and reported it to Google Chrome's team. Google pushed an emergency update to distrust DigiNotar certificates within days, and all major browsers followed. DigiNotar was bankrupt within weeks, and the Dutch government took over its operations. The breach killed the company entirely and prompted the development of Certificate Transparency (CT logs) and CAA DNS records as systemic defenses. It remains the definitive case study for why the CA trust model is the single most critical point of failure in web PKI.
// attacker vs defender mindset
⚔ Attacker
  • A fraudulent CA-signed certificate makes a MitM attack invisible to victims — the padlock shows, no browser warning appears. DigiNotar proved this at nation-state scale.
  • Self-signed certificate errors that users click through are a major attack vector — users trained to ignore warnings create the opening.
  • Certificate transparency logs are now searchable by defenders — attackers must issue certs that will appear there, making stealth harder.
🛡 Defender
  • Certificate Transparency (CT logs): all publicly-trusted certificates are now logged publicly — you can monitor crt.sh for any cert issued for your domains you didn't request.
  • CAA DNS records: specify which CAs are allowed to issue certs for your domain. A simple, underused defense that most organizations skip.
  • HSTS + certificate pinning: forces browsers to only accept your specific certificate or CA — makes fraudulent cert MitM attacks much harder even if a CA is compromised.

See the full certificate chain (Root → Intermediate → Leaf) echo | openssl s_client -connect github.com:443 -showcerts 2>/dev/null | grep "subject\|issuer" subject=CN = github.com issuer=C = US, O = DigiCert, CN = DigiCert TLS RSA SHA256 2020 CA1 subject=CN = DigiCert TLS RSA SHA256 2020 CA1 <- intermediate CA issuer=CN = DigiCert Global Root CA <- root CA # 3 layers: Root (trusted in browser) → Intermediate → Leaf (github.com) # 3. Generate a CSR (what a website sends to a CA) openssl req -new -newkey rsa:2048 -nodes -keyout site.key -out site.csr -subj "/CN=mysite.com/O=MyOrg" Generating a RSA private key... writing CSR # site.key stays on your server FOREVER, site.csr is sent to the CA # 4. Create a self-signed certificate (for labs/internal use) openssl x509 -req -days 365 -in site.csr -signkey site.key -out site.crt Certificate request self-signature ok # browsers will warn "NET::ERR_CERT_AUTHORITY_INVALID" — no trusted CA signed it # 5. Check for CAA records on a domain dig CAA google.com google.com. 300 IN CAA 0 issue "pki.goog" google.com. 300 IN CAA 0 issue "symantec.com" # Google only allows its own CA (pki.goog) to issue certs — CAA record defense
INTERVIEW READY
Q: "How does your browser know a TLS certificate is legitimate?"
A: "It verifies a chain of digital signatures. The server presents a certificate signed by an intermediate CA, which was itself signed by a root CA that your browser already trusts. The browser checks the signature math, the validity dates, that the domain matches, and that the certificate hasn't been revoked via OCSP or CRL. The critical weakness is that any trusted CA can issue certificates for any domain — DigiNotar in 2011 showed this catastrophically: when the Dutch CA was breached, attackers issued a fraudulent Google.com certificate that showed a valid padlock to 300,000 Iranian users who had no way to know it was fake. That incident directly drove the development of Certificate Transparency logs, which force all trusted CAs to publicly log every certificate they issue — making fraudulent certificates detectable."

ENTREPRENEUR ANGLE

Certificate audits are a fast, easy-to-explain win for small business clients. Run SSL Labs on their domain (usually takes 60 seconds), check crt.sh for unauthorized certificates, verify CAA records are set, and confirm auto-renewal is configured for their Let's Encrypt cert. Many SMBs have expired or misconfigured certificates causing customer warnings they don't even know about. A "TLS health check" report is a ₹2,000–₹5,000 quick engagement that demonstrates immediate, visible value — and often opens the door to a broader security audit when they see how much they didn't know.

TEACH IT BACK

Explain to a non-technical person: "Imagine you get a letter claiming to be from your bank, with an official-looking stamp. The stamp is what you trust — if the stamp belongs to an organization you know is legitimate, you trust the letter. TLS certificates work the same way: websites present a 'letter' (certificate) with a 'stamp' from a trusted Certificate Authority. DigiNotar was a trusted stamp-maker that got its stamp stolen in 2011. Attackers used it to send 300,000 fake letters claiming to be from Google, and every recipient thought they were real — because the stamp was genuine."
